abstract: |-
  This paper presents a systematic experimental study of the key factors influencing microwave detection of metallic foreign objects on coal conveyor belts. A dedicated microwave sensor was constructed to investigate the effects of operating frequency, metallic object size, and object–electrode distance on detection performance. Frequency-sweep experiments showed clear frequency-dependent behavior, with the 525–570 MHz band exhibiting strong cross-channel polarity-consistent responses. Using the L1-norm distance of the 32-channel response vector as a sensitivity indicator, 556 MHz was selected as the optimal operating frequency. Experimental results further showed that larger metallic objects produced stronger signal variations, while distance-dependent responses were more evident for larger samples, indicating that the measured response is jointly affected by target size and geometry-dependent field coupling. In addition, microwave tomography based on the linear back-projection algorithm enabled two-dimensional localization and qualitative visualization of aluminum-alloy foreign objects. These findings provide experimental support and practical guidance for operating-frequency selection and sensor design optimization in microwave-based foreign object detection on coal conveyor belts.
